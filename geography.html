Geography of the Western Ghats

Location, mountain ranges, climate, rivers and the important peaks that shape this biodiversity hotspot.

Quick Overview

The Western Ghats (Sahyadri) run parallel to India’s western coast for about 1,600 km, spanning six states. They form a critical climatic barrier that helps drive the monsoon and sustain rich ecosystems across South India.

Location & Range Major Ranges Climate Rivers Important Peaks

Location & Range

The Western Ghats (Sahyadri) extend ~1,600 km from the Tapti River in Gujarat to Kanyakumari in Tamil Nadu. They pass through Gujarat, Maharashtra, Goa, Karnataka, Kerala and Tamil Nadu, forming a steep escarpment facing the Arabian Sea and a gentler slope toward the Deccan Plateau.

  • Length: ~1,600 km
  • States: Gujarat, Maharashtra, Goa, Karnataka, Kerala, Tamil Nadu
  • Role: Monsoon barrier and water catchment for major rivers

Major Mountain Ranges

Sahyadri Range (Maharashtra)

Covers the northern stretch; includes hill stations like Mahabaleshwar and the Kalsubai peak.

Nilgiri Hills

Bordering Tamil Nadu, Kerala and Karnataka — famous for Ooty and montane ecosystems.

Anaimalai (Anamala)

Includes high peaks like Anamudi and large protected areas (Parambikulam, Anamalai).

Cardamom & Agasthyamalai Hills

Southern ranges known for spices, medicinal plants, and unique flora/fauna (Agasthyamala).

Climate

The Western Ghats have a tropical monsoon climate. The western slopes receive heavy southwest monsoon rainfall (June–September), creating wet evergreen forests. Leeward areas get less rain and form drier habitats.

  • High rainfall: some places exceed 5,000–7,000 mm/year.
  • Seasons: Southwest monsoon, northeast monsoon, and dry season.
  • Altitude effect: Higher elevations are cooler with montane shola-grassland ecosystems.

Major Rivers

Many important peninsular rivers originate in the Western Ghats — these rivers feed agriculture, supply drinking water and carve out unique landscapes (falls, gorges).

Kaveri (Cauvery)

Originates in the Kodagu (Coorg) region and flows east into the Bay of Bengal.

Godavari

Major east-flowing river with headwaters in the central Ghats area.

Periyar

Important river of Kerala with Periyar wildlife and lake systems.

Sharavathi

Famous for Jog Falls; flows west toward the Arabian Sea.

Krishna

Another major eastward river sourcing from the Ghats.

Netravati / Tungabhadra (examples)

Numerous tributaries and rivers provide regional water security.

Important Peaks

A quick list of notable peaks — many are popular trekking destinations and form the highest points of the Ghats.

  • Anamudi — 2,695 m (highest in Western Ghats & South India)
  • Meesapulimala — ~2,640 m
  • Doddabetta — ~2,637 m (Nilgiris)
  • Mullayanagiri — ~1,930 m (Karnataka)
  • Kalsubai — ~1,646 m (Maharashtra)
